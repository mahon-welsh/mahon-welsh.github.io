Neuroplasticity and the Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the idea that our brains are malleable and extremely capable of developing new connections. The basic concept is as follows; when practicing or learning things, we develop pathways of neurons that fire when doing that specific thing. With repeated use of these neural pathways, they get stronger, and more streamlined. Eventually our brains have these networks of neurons that fire for specific tasks and knowledge. Interestingly, if tasks or recall happen to be associated, they will have a neural pathway connecting them, and if one of those neurons happens to fire, then the connected neurons are also likely to go off. What this means for us is that when doing things, we are actively strengthening neural connections in our brain, meaning that by consistently thinking about and doing a thing, such as coding, our brain will form stronger pathways, which increases the chance of all the neurons associated with coding to fire, which in turn makes recall and execution more efficient. There are many, many ways to increase neuroplasticity, some studies have even found video games to have a positive effect. Some games require problem solving, while some require puzzle solving and context clues. In terms of more tangible intentional ways to encourage neuroplasticity, one particularly useful source I found offered 9 techniques: Specific diet choices, power naps, not working for too long, working on vocabulary, practice with non-dominant hand, juggling, learning chess, mnemonic drills, and group meditation.

The Growth Mindset

A “fixed mindset” refers to the idea that basic abilities and natural talents are fixed, while obviously people can learn new things, a fixed mindset suggests that one's aptitude for learning and retaining information cannot be shaped. On the contrary, a “growth mindset” is exactly how it sounds. The growth mindset focuses on the process, the idea is to care more about how to solve something than the innate ability to do so. The growth mindset encourages rewards and praise focused on the method and show of resilience rather than praising the actual outcome. In Carol Dweck’s ted talk she discusses how she worked with a team to create a maths learning game that focuses on the growth mindset. It gives encouragement for effort, strategy and progress, rather than when a correct answer is submitted. This game showed significantly higher rates of engagement and perseverance than other similar learning tools. The idea of the yet vs the now is important in the growth mindset. When facing a challenge, don’t think “I’ve failed”, think “I can’t do this yet. This encourages the idea that we can gain capability, and allows us to focus more on the process and trying new solutions. Something I found interesting about this is that the growth and fixed mindsets are attributed to specific parts of life. For instance, with my coding skills, I’ve unconsciously developed a strong growth mindset, whereas with skateboarding, I feel like I’ve always had a fixed mindset, thinking that some people are just born more coordinated and fearless. In terms of my work, I plan to consciously consider the growth mindset when stuck or having difficulties, and take a step back to remind myself that it’s not an “I can’t do this”, it’s an “I can’t do this, yet” situation.

In Relation to my Learning Plan

Since I used to study psychology, the idea of neuroplasticity was already familiar to me, and something that I’ve always tried to apply/work on. However the concept of a growth mindset was new to me. I think my biggest takeaway from this is that some of my limitations aren’t as rigid as I thought. Given I’m neurodiverse, I’ve always thought that focus and time blindness were just inherent issues for me. However, if I were to apply a combination of neuroplasticity concepts and a growth mindset, then I can make realistic steps to improve something that I have predetermined difficulty with.
